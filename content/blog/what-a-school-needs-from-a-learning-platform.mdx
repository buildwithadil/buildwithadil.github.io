Most academies come to me asking for "a website." What they usually need is a
_platform_ — something that markets the school, enrols students, and supports them
through the course. The gap between those two things is where a lot of money gets
wasted. Here's how I'd think it through before spending anything.

## Start with the job, not the features

A learning platform exists to do three jobs: **win enrolments**, **deliver the
course**, and **let your team run it without a developer**. Every feature should
ladder up to one of those. If it doesn't, it's a distraction — however impressive
the demo looked.

## The parts that actually matter

- **A marketing site that builds trust.** Prospective students (and their parents)
  judge you in seconds. Fast, credible, and clear beats flashy.
- **A student portal.** One place to access courses, track progress, and find
  resources. This is the difference between a brochure and a platform.
- **Admin and faculty tools.** Your team needs to add courses, manage students, and
  publish content — themselves, without emailing a developer every time.
- **Enrolment and payments.** If taking money or capturing enquiries is clunky, you
  lose people at the most important moment.
- **Being found.** SEO isn't a nice-to-have for a school; it's an enrolment channel.

## Build vs. buy

Off-the-shelf tools (Teachable, Moodle, and friends) are great when your process
fits theirs. The moment you need your own enrolment flow, your own branding, or a
feature they don't offer, you start paying in workarounds. A custom build costs more
up front but fits your business exactly — and you own it. The right answer depends
on how unusual your process is. I'm happy to tell you honestly when off-the-shelf is
the smarter call.

## The question to ask any developer

"When this is built, can my team run it without you?" If the answer is no, you
haven't bought a platform — you've bought a dependency. Ownership is the whole point.
